# Escalation & Cross-Border Disputes (EU)

When the trader won't resolve it directly, the EU gives consumers structured, free escalation routes. Match the route to whether the trader is in the person's own country or another EU country.

## First: exhaust the trader, in writing

Before escalating, the person should have made a clear written complaint to the trader with a deadline (the dispute message the rest of this skill produces). Escalation bodies expect this, and it builds the record. Keep copies of everything, the order, the complaint, the trader's responses (or silence).

## If the trader is in the person's OWN country

- **National consumer authority / consumer protection body.** Every member state has one (and often a national consumer organisation alongside it). They advise consumers, take complaints, and can act against traders breaching consumer law. This is the primary domestic escalation.
- **Alternative Dispute Resolution (ADR).** Many sectors have an ADR or ombudsman scheme that resolves disputes without court, often free or low-cost, and faster than litigation. Check whether the trader's sector has one.
- **National courts / small-claims procedure.** For higher-value disputes, the local small-claims equivalent is realistic; the national consumer authority can point to the right process.

## If the trader is in ANOTHER EU country (cross-border)

- **European Consumer Centres Network (ECC-Net).** This is the key cross-border resource. There's a European Consumer Centre in each EU country (plus Norway and Iceland). The person contacts the ECC **in their own country**, and the network helps resolve disputes with traders in other member states, explaining rights, mediating with the trader, and pointing to the right ADR or enforcement body. Naming ECC-Net in a dispute (and then actually using it) signals the person knows the cross-border route.
- **European Small Claims Procedure.** For cross-border claims up to a set monetary limit, this standardised EU procedure lets a consumer pursue a trader in another member state without needing a lawyer, using standard forms. Mention it for unresolved cross-border money claims.
- **Online Dispute Resolution.** The consumer can also seek an appropriate ADR entity for online purchases; the national consumer authority or ECC can direct them to the right one.

## The reach point worth stating

EU consumer rights (the right of withdrawal, the legal guarantee) **apply to traders that direct their activities at EU consumers, even if the trader is based outside the EU.** A consumer dealing with a non-EU online seller that ships to the EU is not without rights, though enforcement against a non-EU trader is harder in practice, so the **card chargeback** (read `chargeback.md`) is often the most practical recovery tool in that situation.

## What to tell the person

- For a domestic dispute: complain to the trader in writing, then escalate to the national consumer authority or the relevant ADR scheme; small claims for larger sums.
- For a cross-border dispute: contact the **European Consumer Centre in their own country**, it's free and built precisely for this.
- For any card payment that won't be refunded: run **chargeback** in parallel.
- If the matter is high-value or legally complex, the message is a strong first step but the person should use the consumer authority / ECC and consider local legal help.
