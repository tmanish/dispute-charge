# Legal Guarantee of Conformity: Faulty or Defective Goods (EU)

This is the right for when **something is wrong with the product**, it's faulty, not as described, or doesn't work as it should. It's entirely separate from the 14-day withdrawal right (which is for changing your mind), and it lasts far longer. Don't confuse the two.

## The core right

Under EU law, goods must **conform to the contract**, match the description, be fit for their purpose, and have the qualities and performance the consumer can reasonably expect. If they don't, the consumer is entitled to a **remedy** from the **trader** (the seller, not the manufacturer). This is the **legal guarantee**, and it's mandatory, a trader cannot sign it away in their terms.

## The duration that matters

The EU legal guarantee lasts a **minimum of two years** from delivery. Some member states provide longer, and national rules vary on details, so frame it as "at least two years under the EU legal guarantee, and possibly longer under my national law."

A powerful evidential point: for faults that appear within a defined period after delivery (under EU rules, a substantial part of the guarantee period, at least the first year, longer in some countries), the fault is **presumed to have existed at delivery**, so the **burden is on the trader** to prove otherwise. State this when the fault appeared relatively early, it shifts the argument onto the trader.

## This is separate from any commercial warranty

Don't let a trader fob the person off with "the manufacturer's warranty expired." The **legal guarantee is a statutory right against the seller** and exists regardless of, and in addition to, any commercial/manufacturer warranty the trader or maker offers. A commercial warranty can only add to the consumer's rights, never reduce the legal guarantee.

## The remedies

The consumer is generally entitled to have the goods brought into conformity, typically:
- **Repair or replacement** (usually the consumer's first-tier choice, free of charge and within a reasonable time without significant inconvenience), and
- **A price reduction or a refund** if repair/replacement is impossible, can't be done in reasonable time, fails, or would cause significant inconvenience.

For a serious or persistent fault, the person can push toward refund/price reduction rather than accepting endless repair attempts.

## The facts that win these

- **Evidence of the fault or mismatch**, photos, video, the description vs. what arrived.
- **The delivery date**, it sets the guarantee period and the burden-of-proof window, so pin it down.
- **What was reasonably expected**, the product description, advertised specs, what a reasonable buyer would expect.
- **Any failed repair**, a trader that already tried and failed strengthens the case for refund/replacement.

## Language to include (to the trader)

- "Under the **EU legal guarantee of conformity**, the goods must conform to the contract for at least two years. This item does not conform because [specifics: faulty / not as described / not fit for purpose]."
- "It was delivered on [date]. As the fault appeared within [the relevant period], it is presumed to have existed at delivery, so the burden is on you to show otherwise."
- "I request [repair / replacement] free of charge and within a reasonable time. If that is not possible or fails, I will require a price reduction or refund."
- "Please confirm in writing within [14 days]."

## Escalation

- If the trader refuses, and payment was by card, raise a **chargeback** (read `chargeback.md`).
- Complain to the **national consumer authority** or a relevant **ADR** body.
- For a **cross-border** purchase (trader in another EU country), contact the **European Consumer Centre** (read `escalation-ecc.md`).
- National courts / small-claims equivalents for higher-value disputes; the national consumer authority can advise on the local route.
