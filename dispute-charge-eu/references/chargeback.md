# Chargeback (EU)

Whatever EU statutory right applies, the purchase was almost certainly paid by card, and the **card chargeback** is the practical backstop that often actually recovers the money when a trader won't cooperate. It works alongside the withdrawal right and the legal guarantee, not instead of them.

## What chargeback is (and isn't)

Chargeback is a rule of the **card schemes** (Visa, Mastercard, Amex), not an EU statute. The consumer's card provider can reverse a payment, pulling the funds back from the trader's bank, if it accepts there's a legitimate dispute. Because it's a scheme rule, there's no absolute guarantee, but it's widely available across debit and credit cards and for purchases of essentially any amount.

The right way to use it: rely on the **EU statutory right** (withdrawal or legal guarantee) to establish that the trader must refund, and use **chargeback as the recovery mechanism** when they won't.

## Non-delivery: the EU 30-day default

A frequent case worth handling head-on: **the goods never arrived.** Under the Consumer Rights Directive, unless a different time was agreed, the trader must deliver **within 30 days** of the contract. If they miss the agreed date (or the 30-day default), the consumer can set an **additional reasonable deadline**; if that passes too, or if delivery by the agreed date was essential, the consumer can **terminate the contract and must be refunded all sums paid without undue delay**. The message to the trader should state the order date, the missed deadline, the new deadline being set, and the refund demand. If the trader still won't pay, the chargeback below is the recovery route ("goods not received").

## When to use it

- The trader has accepted (or clearly owes) a refund under the **right of withdrawal** but isn't paying.
- Goods are **faulty / not as described** under the **legal guarantee** and the trader refuses a remedy.
- Goods **never arrived**.
- A payment was taken **after cancellation**, or a **duplicate** charge occurred.
- A refund was **promised but never paid**, or the **trader has ceased trading**.

## The deadline matters

Chargeback has a **time limit**, commonly around **120 days** from the transaction or from when the goods/services were expected (it varies by scheme). Act promptly; if the person is near the limit, the request should go in now.

## The process

1. **Contact the trader first**, chargeback generally expects an attempt to resolve directly, and it builds the paper trail.
2. **If that fails, contact the card provider** (app, online banking, phone, or writing) and ask to raise a **chargeback**, stating the grounds and attaching evidence.
3. The provider claims the funds back from the trader's bank; the trader can contest, so keep documentation.

## Language to include (to the card provider)

- "I would like to raise a **chargeback** on the following transaction."
- Transaction details: date, amount, trader as it appears.
- The grounds: not received / not as described / faulty / charged after cancellation / withdrawal refund unpaid / duplicate / trader ceased trading.
- "I contacted the trader on [date]; [they refused / did not respond / the issue is unresolved]. The trader is obliged to refund me under [my right of withdrawal / the legal guarantee], and has not."
- Attach evidence; request written confirmation of the outcome.

## Escalation

- If the card provider mishandles the chargeback, make a **formal complaint**, then escalate to the relevant **financial dispute / ombudsman scheme** in the person's country.
- Keep the underlying statutory claim alive in parallel, the **national consumer authority** or **European Consumer Centre** (read `escalation-ecc.md`) for the dispute with the trader itself.
