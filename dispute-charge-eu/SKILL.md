# Dispute a Charge (EU)

People lose real money to wrong charges, junk fees, and refused refunds, not because they lack a case, but because the message they send is a polite complaint instead of one that invokes an actual EU right. The EU gives consumers some of the strongest protections in the world; this skill puts them to work.

The goal of every output is a message that does four things a generic complaint doesn't: states the specific facts cleanly, names the mechanism that obligates a response (the right of withdrawal, the legal guarantee of conformity, chargeback, sector rules), sets a deadline, and makes the next escalation visible, the national consumer authority, an ADR body, or the European Consumer Centres Network for cross-border cases, so the recipient knows ignoring it has a cost.

## Two EU rights people constantly confuse: keep them straight

This is the most important thing to get right, because they apply in different situations:

1. **Right of withdrawal (the 14-day "cooling-off" right).** For things bought **at a distance (online, phone, mail order) or off-premises (doorstep)**, the consumer can cancel within **14 calendar days without giving any reason** and get a full refund. This is for *changing your mind*, the item doesn't have to be faulty. Read `right-of-withdrawal.md`.
2. **Legal guarantee of conformity.** For goods that are **faulty, not as described, or not as expected**, the consumer has a separate right to have them **repaired, replaced, or refunded**, and this lasts far longer than 14 days (a minimum of two years in the EU). This is for *something being wrong with the product*. Read `legal-guarantee.md`.

A person who "wants to return an online order they changed their mind about" needs the withdrawal right. A person whose product "broke after three months" needs the legal guarantee. Many disputes are one or the other; pick correctly.

## The one rule that matters most

**Get the facts before writing.** A vague message gets a form reply; a specific one gets resolved. If the key facts are missing, ask for them first, efficiently, in one batch:
- **What** was bought/charged, **how much**, and the **exact date** (of order, delivery, or charge)
- **Who** the trader is, and **how it was bought** (online / phone / doorstep / in a shop, this decides whether the withdrawal right even applies)
- **Why** it's wrong (changed mind within 14 days / faulty / not as described / not delivered / cancelled but charged / unauthorised)
- **What they've already done** (contacted the trader? reference number? been refused?)
- **What outcome they want** (refund, repair, replacement)
- **Which country** the person and the trader are in (decides national authority and whether it's a cross-border ECC-Net case)

If facts are missing but the person wants speed, write with bracketed placeholders like `[date of delivery]`.

## Structure of an effective dispute message

Keep it tight, one page. Long messages read as emotional; short ones read as serious.

```
[Your name]
[Your address / contact]
[Order or reference number]
[Date]

To: [Trader / customer service / card provider]

Re: [Withdrawal / refund request / claim], [amount] dated [date]

[1. The claim, in one or two sentences. What was bought/charged, when, and
what you want (to withdraw / a refund / repair). State amount and date precisely.]

[2. The facts, in plain chronological order, order number, delivery date,
cancellation date, prior contact, evidence.]

[3. The mechanism. The right that applies and what it obligates. E.g. "I am
exercising my right of withdrawal under the Consumer Rights Directive; you must
refund all payments, including standard delivery, within 14 days."]

[4. The ask + deadline, exactly what you want and by when.]

[5. The escalation, stated calmly, the national consumer authority, an ADR
body, or the European Consumer Centre for cross-border disputes.]

[Signature]
```

## Tone: calm, specific, slightly formal

- **No insults, no capitals, no emotional escalation**, anger gives the recipient a reason to dismiss you.
- **No over-explaining or apologising**, state the fact: "This charge is incorrect" / "I am withdrawing from this contract."
- **Cite specifics relentlessly**, dates, amounts, order numbers.
- **One clear ask**, stated plainly and once.
- **Name the deadline and the next step.**

## How to deliver the output

Default to a ready-to-send message the person can copy, most EU disputes start by email, the trader's online form, or (increasingly) a mandatory online withdrawal function on the trader's site. A clear written statement is enough to exercise the withdrawal right; the consumer doesn't have to use the trader's model form (though it's fine to). If the person seems unsure how to send it, point them to the best channel.

## A note on reach and refunds

Two facts worth weaving in where relevant:
- **EU consumer law binds traders that target EU consumers, wherever the trader is based.** A non-EU online seller shipping to an EU consumer is still bound by these rights. The person isn't powerless just because the company is abroad.
- **When the withdrawal right is used, the trader must refund *all* payments received, including the standard delivery cost** (not enhanced/express delivery the consumer chose), normally within **14 days**, though the trader may withhold the refund until the goods are returned or proof of return is provided. The consumer usually pays the *return* postage unless the trader didn't inform them of this or agreed to cover it.

## What this skill does not do

- It does not invent law or get specific where it shouldn't. National implementations vary; where unsure of a country-specific detail, describe the EU-level right and point the person to their national consumer authority rather than asserting a precise national provision. A confidently wrong citation hurts the case.
- It does not assume the law is frozen. Consumer rules change; if the dispute hinges on a specific rule, threshold, or a very recent regulation you're not fully certain is current, verify with a quick web search (if available) before citing it, and if you can't verify, describe the mechanism generically rather than asserting the detail.
- It does not apply EU law to non-EU people. If it becomes clear the person is not in the EU/EEA and isn't buying from an EU-targeting trader, do not cite the withdrawal right or the legal guarantee. Fall back to the universal structure, exact facts, the contract/policy terms, one clear ask, a deadline, visible escalation, plus the near-universal card chargeback, and point them to their own country's consumer authority for the specific local mechanism.
- It does not give formal legal advice or guarantee outcomes. For complex or high-value matters, say plainly the message is a strong first step and point the person to their national consumer authority, an ADR body, or the European Consumer Centre.
- It does not help fabricate facts. The strategy depends on a truthful account; disputing in bad faith can be fraud. Redirect to disputes the person actually has grounds for.

## Quick reference: the highest-impact EU moves

1. **Name the exact transaction** (date + amount + trader). Vagueness is the #1 reason disputes fail.
2. **Pick the right right:** withdrawal (14-day cooling-off, changed mind, distance/off-premises) vs. legal guarantee (faulty, ≥2 years). Don't mix them up.
3. **Set a written deadline**, and remember the trader's refund clock is 14 days for withdrawal.
4. **Use the reach point**, remind a foreign trader that targeting EU consumers means EU rules apply.
5. **Escalate via ECC-Net for cross-border**, naming it shows you know the route. Keep it to one page and zero emotion.
