# Subscription, Free-Trial, and "I Cancelled But Got Billed" Disputes (UK)

A very common situation, and one where the UK approach combines the person's contract/cancellation evidence with the card-payment levers. Run it as a two-track play.

## The framing

UK consumers have protections around subscriptions and auto-renewals through general consumer law (the Consumer Rights Act, unfair-terms rules, and rules against misleading practices), and the regulatory direction has been toward making subscriptions easier to exit and requiring clear pre-contract information and reminders. Rather than cite a single specific provision you may not have current details on, lead with the **facts and the contract**: did the person cancel, when, and were they charged anyway? That's the spine. If you reference the regulatory backdrop, keep it general ("rules on subscription contracts and unfair terms") rather than asserting a precise statutory section or date you're unsure of.

## The facts that win these

- **The cancellation evidence.** Did they cancel? When? Confirmation email, screenshot, or reference number? "I cancelled on 12 February (confirmation #X) and was still charged on 3 March" is close to unbeatable.
- **The sign-up vs. cancel asymmetry.** If sign-up was two clicks but cancellation required calling a line that's never answered, that asymmetry is itself the grievance, document it.
- **The free-trial conversion.** If the conversion from free to paid wasn't clearly disclosed or consented to, say so specifically.

## Two-track strategy (the key move)

Run **both** at once:

1. **Tell the merchant**, a clear, dated message demanding a refund of the specific charge, citing the cancellation evidence, with a deadline.
2. **Use the card/bank lever in parallel**, because a subscription is charged to a card or account:
   - Credit card → **chargeback** for "charged after cancellation," and **Section 75** if the broader contract value brings it into the £100.01–£30,000 band (read `section-75.md` and `chargeback.md`).
   - Debit card → **chargeback** (read `chargeback.md`).
   - The chargeback is often what actually claws the money back, while the merchant message builds the record.

**Stop the bleeding first:** if it's a recurring charge, cancel through every available channel now. The person can also instruct their **bank to cancel a Continuous Payment Authority (CPA)**, under UK rules the bank must stop the payments when asked, even if the merchant keeps trying to collect. This is an important, under-used point: tell the person they can go to their bank to block further subscription charges directly, and the bank is obliged to act.

## Language to include (merchant message)

- "I'm requesting a refund of the £[amount] charge dated [date]. I cancelled this subscription on [date] (confirmation [#]), so this charge should not have been taken."
- Where it fits: "Continuing to charge me after cancellation is not consistent with my cancellation and the terms that applied."
- "Please refund £[amount] to my original payment method within [14 days] and confirm the subscription is fully cancelled with no further charges."
- Escalation line: "If this isn't resolved, I'll raise a chargeback with my card provider and instruct my bank to cancel the payment authority, and I may refer the matter to the Financial Ombudsman."

## Escalation

- **Card chargeback** (the parallel track), often the fastest actual refund.
- **Instruct the bank to cancel the CPA** to stop future charges.
- **Financial Ombudsman Service** if a bank or card provider mishandles the related dispute (after 8 weeks / final response).
- **Citizens Advice / Trading Standards** for a trader engaging in misleading or aggressive subscription practices.
