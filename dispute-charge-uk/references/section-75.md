# Section 75 of the Consumer Credit Act 1974

This is the strongest consumer lever in the UK and the one most people don't know to use. It applies to **credit card** purchases and makes the card provider **jointly and severally liable** with the retailer if something goes wrong, which means the person can claim the full amount back from the card company, not just the trader.

## When Section 75 applies

All of these must be true:
- Paid (at least partly) by **credit card**, not debit, not a charge card that must be settled in full each month, not Buy Now Pay Later. (Some store cards, store instalment credit, and PayPal Credit can also qualify as they're forms of credit; the card provider's terms govern edge cases.)
- The **cash price of the single item or service is over £100 and no more than £30,000.** It's the price of the thing that matters, not how much went on the card.
- There is a **direct debtor–creditor–supplier link**, the person bought directly from the supplier using their credit card.

## The two rules people don't know (and that win cases)

1. **The 1p rule.** You only need to have paid *part* of the price on the credit card, even a £1 deposit, for the *entire* purchase to be covered, as long as the total cash price is in the £100.01–£30,000 band. So a £2,000 sofa where only the £200 deposit went on the credit card is fully covered.
2. **You can claim against the card provider directly.** You do *not* have to exhaust the retailer first or reach deadlock. You can claim from the retailer, the card provider, or both at once (though you can't recover the same loss twice). This is the killer feature when the **retailer has gone bust, ignores you, or refuses**, the card provider is on the hook regardless.

## What it covers

A **breach of contract** or **misrepresentation** by the supplier:
- Goods not delivered
- Goods faulty, not as described, or not fit for purpose
- Services not performed, or not as promised
- The trader misrepresented what was being sold
- The trader has gone into administration before delivering

Associated/consequential losses can sometimes be recovered too (e.g. if a hotel loses your booking, the cost of alternative last-minute accommodation may be claimable).

## The crucial limitation: third-party payment processors

Section 75 **breaks if a third party sits between the buyer and the supplier** in the payment chain, for example, paying through some third-party payment services or buying via certain marketplaces where you don't pay the supplier directly. This snaps the direct debtor–creditor–supplier link the law requires. If that's the situation, Section 75 may not apply, pivot to **chargeback** (read `chargeback.md`), which doesn't have this restriction. Be honest about this rather than raising the person's hopes on a claim that won't qualify.

(There is also "Section 75A", a weaker, "second in line" protection for certain larger linked-credit purchases, but don't over-rely on it; if the standard Section 75 conditions aren't met, chargeback is usually the more practical route.)

## No tight deadline, but act promptly

Unlike chargeback, there's **no short statutory time limit** on a Section 75 claim. In practice, claims are usually brought within the six-year limitation period for breach of contract (from the date of the breach/misrepresentation). Still, the sooner the better, evidence and memories fade.

## Language to include

- "I am making a claim under **Section 75 of the Consumer Credit Act 1974**."
- State the purchase: item/service, supplier, date, cash price, and that you paid (at least partly) by credit card.
- State the breach or misrepresentation precisely (not delivered / faulty / not as described / supplier insolvent).
- "As my credit provider, you are jointly and severally liable with the supplier for this breach. I am therefore claiming £[amount] from you."
- Provide supporting evidence: order confirmation, correspondence with the retailer, photos, proof the retailer has ceased trading if relevant.
- "Please refund £[amount] within [14 days] and confirm in writing."

## Escalation

- If the card provider rejects the claim or doesn't respond, make a **formal complaint** to them in writing.
- If unresolved after **8 weeks**, or if they issue a final response you disagree with, escalate to the **Financial Ombudsman Service** (free, independent, decisions can bind the provider). This is the standard and effective next step, name it in the letter.
- For amounts or issues beyond that, the **County Court small claims track** is realistic for many of these sums; Citizens Advice can help the person prepare.
