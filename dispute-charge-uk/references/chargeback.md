# Chargeback (UK)

Chargeback is the workhorse that fills the gaps Section 75 leaves. It applies to **debit cards, credit cards, and charge cards** and to purchases of **any amount**, including under £100, where Section 75 can't reach. It's how most everyday card disputes actually get resolved.

## What chargeback is (and isn't)

Chargeback is **not a legal right**, it's a rule of the card schemes (Visa, Mastercard, Amex). The person's card provider can reverse a payment, pulling the money back from the retailer's bank, if it agrees there's a legitimate dispute. Because it's a scheme rule rather than a statute, there's no absolute guarantee, but in practice it's widely used and often the fastest route to a refund.

Frame it honestly: chargeback is generally considered *lesser* protection than Section 75 (which is law and makes the card provider jointly liable), but it covers situations Section 75 can't, debit-card payments, purchases under £100, and cases where a third-party payment processor breaks the Section 75 link.

## When to use chargeback

- Paid by **debit card** (Section 75 never applies to debit).
- The item/service cost **£100 or less** (below the Section 75 floor).
- A **third-party processor** sat between buyer and supplier, breaking the Section 75 link.
- As a **quicker first attempt** even on Section 75-eligible purchases, though for credit-card purchases in the £100.01–£30,000 band, Section 75 is the stronger fallback if chargeback fails.

Common grounds: goods not received, goods not as described or faulty, a payment taken after you cancelled, a duplicate charge, a refund promised but never paid, or the retailer has ceased trading.

## The deadline matters

Chargeback has a **time limit**, typically around **120 days** from the transaction date or from when you expected to receive the goods/services (it can vary by card scheme, sometimes 45–120 days). This is much tighter than Section 75's lack of a short deadline, so **act promptly**. If the person is near the limit, the message should go in now.

## The process

1. **Contact the retailer first.** Chargeback generally expects the person to try to resolve it with the merchant before raising it. This also builds the paper trail.
2. **If that fails, contact the card provider**, via the app, online banking, phone, or in writing, and ask to raise a **chargeback**, stating the grounds and providing evidence.
3. The provider requests the money back from the retailer's bank; the retailer can contest it, so keep documentation.

## Language to include (to the card provider)

- "I would like to raise a **chargeback** on the following transaction."
- Transaction details: date, amount, merchant as it appears.
- The grounds: not received / not as described / faulty / charged after cancellation / duplicate / retailer ceased trading.
- "I contacted the retailer on [date] to resolve this and [they refused / did not respond / the issue remains unresolved]."
- Attach evidence and ask for written confirmation of the outcome.

## Escalation

- If the card provider mishandles the chargeback, make a **formal complaint** to them, then escalate to the **Financial Ombudsman Service** after 8 weeks or on a final response.
- For credit-card purchases that fail chargeback but meet the £100.01–£30,000 band, fall back to a **Section 75 claim** (read `section-75.md`), it's the stronger, statutory route.
