# Consumer Rights Act 2015 (faulty / not as described / not fit for purpose)

This is the statute that governs the *quality* of what a person buys from a trader, and it sits underneath many disputes. Where Section 75 and chargeback are about getting money back *through the card provider*, the Consumer Rights Act is about the person's rights *against the trader directly*, and it's often what establishes that the trader breached in the first place.

## What the Act requires

Goods sold by a trader to a consumer must be:
- **Of satisfactory quality** (not faulty, not damaged, meeting the standard a reasonable person would expect)
- **As described** (matching the description, sample, or model shown)
- **Fit for purpose** (including any particular purpose the consumer made known)

Services must be performed with **reasonable care and skill**, within a reasonable time, and for a reasonable price if none was agreed. Digital content has its own equivalent quality standards.

## The remedies, and the timing that matters

The Act gives a tiered set of remedies, and the **timeline determines which apply**:
- **Within 30 days** of purchase: for faulty goods, the consumer has a **short-term right to reject** and get a **full refund**. This is a strong, clean right, name the 30-day window if the person is inside it.
- **After 30 days but within 6 months**: the consumer is generally entitled to a **repair or replacement**. If that fails (or isn't possible), they can claim a refund, which may be reduced to reflect use. Importantly, faults appearing in the first 6 months are generally **presumed to have been present at purchase**, so the burden is on the trader to prove otherwise, a useful point to state.
- **After 6 months**: the consumer can still have rights, but the burden shifts to them to show the fault was inherent.

## Non-delivery: the 30-day default

A frequent case worth handling head-on: **the goods never arrived.** Under the Consumer Rights Act framework, unless a different date was agreed, the trader must deliver **within 30 days**. If they miss the agreed date (or the 30-day default), the consumer can set an **additional reasonable deadline**; if that passes too, or if delivery by the agreed date was essential (e.g. a wedding dress for the wedding), the consumer can **treat the contract as at an end and get a full refund**. The letter should state the order date, the missed deadline, the new deadline being set (or that time was of the essence), and the refund demand. If the trader won't pay, recover via **Section 75** or **chargeback** as usual.

## Why this pairs with Section 75 / chargeback

The Consumer Rights Act establishes **that the trader breached** (the goods were faulty/not as described). Section 75 and chargeback are the *routes to recover the money*, especially when the trader won't cooperate. So for a faulty item bought on a credit card, the winning letter does both: it asserts the Consumer Rights Act breach *and* invokes Section 75 against the card provider. Read `section-75.md` (credit card) or `chargeback.md` (debit/smaller) alongside this file and combine them.

## The facts that win these

- **Evidence of the fault or mismatch**, photos, video, the product description vs. what arrived.
- **The purchase date**, it determines which remedy tier applies, so pin it down.
- **What was made known to the trader**, if the consumer told the trader they needed the item for a specific purpose and it doesn't meet it, "fit for purpose" applies.
- **Any failed repair/replacement**, if the trader already tried and failed, that unlocks the refund route after 30 days.

## Language to include (to the trader)

- "Under the **Consumer Rights Act 2015**, goods must be of satisfactory quality, as described, and fit for purpose. This item is [faulty / not as described / not fit for purpose] because [specifics]."
- If within 30 days: "As this is within 30 days of purchase, I am exercising my short-term right to reject and requesting a full refund of £[amount]."
- If 30 days–6 months: "I am requesting a [repair / replacement]. As the fault appeared within six months of purchase, it is presumed to have been present at the point of sale."
- "Please confirm in writing within [14 days]."

## Escalation

- If the trader refuses, and the purchase was on a card, escalate via **Section 75** (credit card) or **chargeback** (debit/smaller), read those files.
- An **Alternative Dispute Resolution (ADR)** scheme may apply to the trader's sector.
- **Citizens Advice** (and the Citizens Advice consumer service) can help, and they pass evidence to **Trading Standards**.
- The **County Court small claims track** is realistic for many of these amounts.
