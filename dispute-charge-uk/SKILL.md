# Dispute a Charge (UK)

People lose real money every year to wrong charges, junk fees, and refused refunds, not because they don't have a case, but because the letter they write is a polite complaint instead of a request that invokes an actual legal right. This skill turns a frustrated person into someone the company has to take seriously, using the mechanisms that exist under UK law.

The goal of every output is a message that does four things a generic complaint doesn't: states the specific facts cleanly, names the mechanism that obligates a response (Section 75, chargeback, the Consumer Rights Act, sector rules), sets a deadline, and makes the next escalation visible, usually the Financial Ombudsman Service or the relevant regulator, so the recipient knows ignoring it has a cost.

## The one rule that matters most

**Get the facts before writing the letter.** A vague letter ("you charged me wrong, please fix it") gets a form reply. A specific letter ("on 3 March my account was charged £49.99 by MERCHANT for a subscription I cancelled on 12 February, confirmation #A1234") gets resolved. If the person hasn't given you the key facts, ask for them first, efficiently, in one batch, and only for what you actually need.

The facts that almost always matter:
- **What** was charged and **how much** (exact amount, exact date)
- **Who** charged it (merchant name as it appears, card type if relevant)
- **Why it's wrong** (never received, cancelled, duplicate, wrong amount, unauthorised, not as described, faulty)
- **What they've already done** (contacted the trader? got a reference number? been refused? first contact?)
- **What outcome they want** (full refund, partial, correction, repair/replacement)
- **How they paid** (this is decisive in the UK, credit card unlocks Section 75; debit/credit both allow chargeback)

If some facts are missing but the person wants to move fast, write the letter with bracketed placeholders like `[exact date of charge]` so they can fill the gaps rather than stalling.

Often two apply (e.g. a faulty £400 item bought on a credit card → Section 75 *and* Consumer Rights Act). Read both and combine: the Consumer Rights Act establishes *that* the trader breached, and Section 75 lets the person claim against the card provider too. ## Structure of an effective dispute letter

Use this skeleton. Keep it tight, one page is the target. Long letters read as emotional; short letters read as serious.

```
[Your name]
[Your address]
[Account / card last 4 digits, or order/reference number]
[Date]

To: [Company / disputes team / card provider]

Re: [Refund request / Section 75 claim / dispute], [amount] dated [date]

[1. The claim, in one or two sentences. What was charged, when, and that
you are disputing it or claiming a refund. State the amount and date precisely.]

[2. The facts. What happened, in plain chronological order. Include order
numbers, cancellation dates, prior contact references, evidence. The spine,
specific and unemotional.]

[3. The mechanism. State the right that applies (from the reference file) and
what it obligates. E.g. "Under Section 75 of the Consumer Credit Act 1974, you
as my credit provider are jointly liable with the retailer for this breach."]

[4. The ask + deadline. State exactly what you want (refund the full [amount])
and by when (within [14 days]).]

[5. The escalation, stated calmly. Name the next step if unresolved, the
Financial Ombudsman Service after 8 weeks, the relevant regulator, etc.]

[Signature]
```

## Tone: calm, specific, slightly formal

The most effective disputes sound like they were written by someone who knows their rights and fully expects to win, not someone angry, and not someone begging. Concretely:
- **No insults, no capitals, no emotional escalation.** Anger gives the recipient a reason to dismiss you.
- **No over-explaining or apologising.** State the fact: "This charge is incorrect."
- **Cite specifics relentlessly.** Dates, amounts, names, order/reference numbers.
- **One clear ask**, stated plainly and once.
- **Name the deadline and the next step.** A letter that says "within 14 days, or I will refer this to the Financial Ombudsman" cannot simply be filed away.

## How to deliver the output

Default to a ready-to-send message the person can copy directly, most UK disputes start by email, secure message, or the card provider's app or online form. For a formal Section 75 claim, a written claim to the card provider (letter or their formal claims route) is the right channel; the reference file notes this. If the person seems unsure how to send it, briefly tell them the best channel for their situation.

## Use the 8-week / Ombudsman rhythm

A specific UK feature worth building into the strategy: for disputes with banks and card providers (including Section 75 and chargeback), if the firm doesn't resolve it or you're unhappy with the outcome, you can escalate to the **Financial Ombudsman Service (FOS)**, generally after the firm has had **8 weeks**, or sooner if they issue a final response / deadlock letter. FOS is free, independent, and its decisions can bind the firm. Mentioning FOS in the letter signals the person knows the escalation path. (For non-financial disputes, the escalation is the relevant regulator, an Alternative Dispute Resolution / ombudsman scheme, or the small claims track of the County Court.)

## What this skill does not do

- It does not invent law or thresholds. The Section 75 band is £100.01–£30,000 on the cash price; the chargeback window is typically ~120 days; don't state a number you're unsure of, describe the mechanism instead. A confidently wrong citation hurts the case.
- It does not assume the law is frozen. Consumer rules change; if the dispute hinges on a specific rule, threshold, or a very recent regulation you're not fully certain is current, verify with a quick web search (if available) before citing it, and if you can't verify, describe the mechanism generically rather than asserting the detail.
- It does not apply UK law to non-UK people. If it becomes clear the person is not in the UK (or the trader relationship isn't UK-governed), do not cite Section 75, the Consumer Rights Act, or FOS. Fall back to the universal structure, exact facts, the contract/policy terms, one clear ask, a deadline, visible escalation, plus the near-universal card chargeback, and point them to their own country's consumer authority for the specific local mechanism.
- It does not give formal legal advice or guarantee outcomes. For genuinely complex or high-value matters (large sums, anything heading to court or already in debt collection), say plainly the letter is a strong first step but the person may want Citizens Advice, a solicitor, or the relevant ombudsman, and point them there.
- It does not help fabricate facts. The strategy depends on a truthful, documented account. Disputing in bad faith (e.g. claiming non-delivery for something received and used) can be fraud, redirect to disputes the person actually has grounds for.

## Quick reference: the highest-impact UK moves

1. **Name the exact transaction** (date + amount + merchant). Vagueness is the #1 reason disputes fail.
2. **Pick the right lever:** Section 75 for credit-card purchases £100.01–£30,000; chargeback for debit or smaller; Consumer Rights Act for faulty/not-as-described.
3. **Set a written deadline**, "within 14 days" beats "as soon as possible."
4. **Invoke the Ombudsman path** for bank/card disputes, naming FOS and the 8-week point shows you know the route.
5. **Keep it to one page and zero emotion.** Serious cases read as calm and specific.
