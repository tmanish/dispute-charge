# Subscription, Free-Trial, and "I Cancelled But Got Billed" Disputes

This is one of the most common money-back situations and one where the right framing matters, because the legal landscape shifted recently and it's easy to cite a rule that no longer applies.

## Get the framing right (important accuracy note)

Do **not** cite the FTC "Click-to-Cancel" rule as current law. It was vacated by a federal appeals court in July 2025 and is not in effect. Citing a dead rule undermines the person's credibility.

What *is* in force and can be invoked:
- **ROSCA (Restore Online Shoppers' Confidence Act).** For anything signed up for online with recurring billing, ROSCA requires the seller to have clearly disclosed all material terms before charging, gotten the consumer's express informed consent, and provided a simple way to stop recurring charges. If the person was charged after a "free trial" they didn't realize would convert, or couldn't find a way to cancel, ROSCA is the relevant federal lever.
- **FTC Act Section 5**, prohibits unfair or deceptive practices. Making cancellation much harder than sign-up, or misrepresenting a subscription as easy to cancel, can be an unfair/deceptive practice. The FTC actively brings these cases, including recent enforcement actions against major subscription and membership programs over hard-to-cancel flows.
- **State automatic-renewal laws (ARLs).** Many states (California, New York, and a growing list) have their own auto-renewal statutes, some stricter than federal rules, requiring clear renewal disclosures, affirmative consent, and easy online cancellation, sometimes with reminder notices before renewal. These remain fully enforceable. If the person is in one of these states, it strengthens the case. (Don't claim a specific state has a specific provision unless you're confident, frame it as "your state's automatic-renewal law may require..." and point them to their state attorney general.)

Note on currency: the FTC reopened rulemaking on this topic in 2026 through an Advance Notice of Proposed Rulemaking, signaling it wants a broader rule again. As of this writing that process has not produced any new rule in effect. If this dispute is happening well after this file was written, verify with a search whether a new rule has since taken effect before citing anything beyond ROSCA, Section 5, and state ARLs.

## The facts that win these

- **The cancellation evidence.** Did they cancel? When? Is there a confirmation email, screenshot, or reference number? This is the spine of the dispute. "I cancelled on February 12, confirmation #X, and was still charged on March 3" is close to unbeatable.
- **The sign-up vs. cancel asymmetry.** If they signed up in two clicks but cancellation required calling a number that's never answered or mailing a form, that asymmetry is itself the violation. Document it.
- **The free-trial conversion.** If the conversion from free to paid wasn't clearly disclosed, or consent wasn't clearly obtained, say so specifically.

## Two-track strategy (this is the key move for subscriptions)

For a wrong subscription charge, run **both** tracks at once, they reinforce each other:

1. **Tell the merchant**, a clear, dated message demanding a refund of the specific charge, citing the cancellation evidence and (where it fits) ROSCA / the state auto-renewal law, with a deadline.
2. **Dispute with the card issuer or bank in parallel**, because a subscription is charged to a card or account, the payment-method mechanism applies too. For a credit card, read `credit-card.md` (FCBA, and unauthorized-charge rules if the charge came after cancellation). For debit, read `bank-debit.md` (Regulation E). The chargeback is often what actually claws the money back, while the merchant message creates the paper trail.

Tell the person to **stop the bleeding first**: if it's a recurring charge, cancel through every available channel immediately and, if needed, tell the bank to block future payments from that merchant, otherwise they keep getting charged while the dispute runs.

## Language to include (merchant message)

- "I'm requesting a refund of the [amount] charge dated [date]. I cancelled this subscription on [date] (confirmation [#]), so this charge should not have occurred."
- Where it fits: "Charging me after cancellation, and/or making cancellation substantially harder than sign-up, is inconsistent with the Restore Online Shoppers' Confidence Act and may violate my state's automatic-renewal law."
- "Please refund [amount] to my original payment method within [10 days] and confirm the subscription is fully cancelled with no further charges."
- The escalation line: "If this isn't resolved, I'll dispute the charge with my card issuer and file a complaint with the FTC and my state attorney general."

## Escalation

- **Card/bank chargeback** (the parallel track above), often the fastest actual refund.
- **FTC** at reportfraud.ftc.gov, the FTC weights subscription/cancellation complaints heavily right now.
- **State attorney general's consumer protection office**, especially powerful where a state ARL applies.
- **CFPB** (consumerfinance.gov/complaint) if a bank or card issuer mishandles the related dispute.
