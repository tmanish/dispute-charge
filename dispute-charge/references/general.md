# General Disputes & Outside the United States

Use this file when the situation doesn't fit the other categories, or when the person is **outside the United States** (where the US-specific mechanisms in the other files. FCBA, Regulation E, ROSCA, No Surprises Act, don't apply and must not be cited).

## The universal structure works everywhere

Regardless of country or charge type, an effective dispute does the same five things. This structure is the real engine; the specific legal citation is a booster, not a requirement.

1. **State the exact charge**, date, amount, merchant, account/reference. Precision signals a documented case.
2. **State plainly why it's wrong**, unauthorized, duplicate, wrong amount, not received, cancelled, not as described.
3. **Name a mechanism if you genuinely have one**, the right chargeback/consumer-protection route for that country (below), or simply the merchant's own refund policy and the contract terms. If you're not certain a specific law applies, don't cite it, lean on the facts and the contract instead.
4. **State the outcome and a deadline**, "refund [amount] within [X] days."
5. **Make escalation visible**, the next step (chargeback, regulator, ombudsman, public review) so inaction has a cost.

## Mechanisms by region (use only what you're confident about)

Don't invent statute numbers. Where you're unsure of the current specifics, name the *type* of mechanism and point the person to the right body rather than citing a law precisely.

- **United Kingdom:** For card purchases, **chargeback** (via the card scheme) applies broadly. For credit-card purchases of a certain value, **Section 75 of the Consumer Credit Act** can make the card issuer jointly liable with the seller, a strong lever for non-delivery or misrepresentation. The **Financial Ombudsman Service** handles unresolved disputes with banks/card issuers. General consumer rights sit under the Consumer Rights Act.
- **European Union:** Card **chargeback** via the scheme. Strong statutory rights for online purchases including a **14-day cooling-off / withdrawal right** for many distance contracts. National consumer authorities and the European Consumer Centres Network (ECC-Net) for cross-border issues.
- **Canada:** Card **chargeback** via the network. Provincial consumer-protection laws govern refunds and unfair practices; the relevant **provincial consumer affairs office** is the escalation.
- **Australia:** Card **chargeback**. **Australian Consumer Law** gives strong guarantee/refund rights; **state/territory fair-trading offices** and the **ACCC** are the escalation points.
- **Elsewhere / unsure:** Default to the **card chargeback** (almost universal for card payments), the merchant's own policy and contract, and the country's consumer-protection authority or banking ombudsman. Tell the person you're giving the universal approach and suggest they confirm the specific local mechanism with their consumer authority.

## The card chargeback is nearly universal

For almost any card payment in any country, the **chargeback through the card issuer** is the most broadly available lever. The basic move: ask the issuer to reverse the charge, citing the reason (unauthorized / not received / not as described / cancelled). Networks set time limits (often around 120 days, sometimes longer), so act promptly. Run the merchant request first to build the record, then chargeback if the merchant doesn't resolve it.

## Language to include

- "I'm disputing a charge of [amount] dated [date] from [merchant], reference [#], because [reason]."
- "[Under my card issuer's chargeback process / under the merchant's stated refund policy / under (named local mechanism if confident)], please reverse this charge and refund [amount]."
- "Please resolve this within [X] days and confirm in writing."
- "If unresolved, I'll pursue a chargeback with my card issuer and a complaint with [the relevant consumer authority / ombudsman]."

## When to tell the person to get help

Be honest about limits. If the matter is high-value, involves a contract dispute beyond a simple refund, has gone to debt collection, or turns on local law you're not sure of, say plainly: this letter is a strong first step, but they should confirm the specific mechanism with their national/state consumer-protection authority or banking ombudsman, and for large amounts consider a consumer-law professional.
