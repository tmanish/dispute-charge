# Medical and Hospital Bill Disputes

Medical bills are different from other charges in a crucial way: **they are wrong remarkably often**, and the dispute strategy is less about a single statute and more about forcing transparency, checking the bill against what was actually owed, and using the specific protections that do exist. Approach these methodically, the amounts are often large enough that getting it right matters a lot.

## First move, always: get the itemized bill

Most medical bills arrive as a summary ("Services: $4,200"). That summary is not enough to dispute or even verify. **Request a fully itemized bill** with every line item, procedure code (CPT/HCPCS codes), and charge. This is the single highest-leverage first step, for three reasons:
- Itemized bills routinely reveal errors: duplicate charges, services never received, wrong quantities, upcoding (billing a more expensive code than the service warranted).
- The provider must be able to substantiate each line.
- It buys time and signals the person is going to scrutinize the bill, which often prompts a review on the provider's side.

The person has a right to request this. The message should ask for the itemized bill in writing and note that they're reviewing the charges before payment.

## Check the bill against three things

1. **What was actually done.** Compare line items to the care received. Charges for a test never run, a room never occupied, a second day not stayed, these are common and disputable on the facts.
2. **The insurance Explanation of Benefits (EOB).** If insured, the EOB shows what the insurer allowed, paid, and what the patient actually owes. A provider bill that exceeds the EOB's patient-responsibility amount is often an error or improper balance-billing. The phrase to use: "This bill doesn't match my Explanation of Benefits."
3. **Whether it should have been covered / was billed in-network.** Claim-coding errors and wrong network status cause huge overbills. If a claim was denied or misprocessed, the fight may actually be with the insurer (an appeal), not the provider.

## Specific protections worth naming

- **No Surprises Act.** Federal law protects patients from many surprise out-of-network bills, particularly for emergency care, and for out-of-network providers at in-network facilities (e.g. an out-of-network anesthesiologist at an in-network hospital). If the bill is a surprise out-of-network charge in one of these situations, this is a strong, specific lever, name it. (Be honest about its limits: it doesn't cover every situation, e.g. ground ambulances are largely excluded.)
- **Good Faith Estimate.** Uninsured/self-pay patients are entitled to a good-faith estimate of costs in advance; if the final bill substantially exceeds it, there's a dispute process. If the person got an estimate and the bill blew past it, that's a concrete basis to push back.
- **Insurance appeal rights.** If the real problem is a denied or underpaid claim, the patient (or provider on their behalf) can appeal to the insurer, and after internal appeals, request an external review. Frame this as the right track when the issue is coverage rather than the provider's arithmetic.

## Before paying anything

- **Don't pay a disputed medical bill in full just to make it stop**, if the person believes it's wrong, paying can complicate getting money back. (Paying the clearly-correct portion while disputing the rest is reasonable.)
- **Watch the collections clock**, but know that medical debt has gained some specific protections in recent years regarding credit reporting. If a bill is heading to collections, that raises the stakes and may warrant faster escalation or professional help, say so.
- **Ask about financial assistance / charity care.** Nonprofit hospitals are generally required to have financial-assistance policies; many patients qualify and never ask. This isn't a "dispute" but it's often the fastest path to a smaller bill, so mention it where the person's issue is affordability rather than error.

## Language to include

- "Please send me a fully itemized bill listing each service, procedure code, and charge. I am reviewing these charges before remitting payment."
- If applicable: "This bill does not match the patient-responsibility amount on my Explanation of Benefits dated [date]. Please review and correct."
- If applicable: "This appears to be a surprise out-of-network charge covered by the No Surprises Act, as [emergency care / out-of-network provider at an in-network facility]. Please reprocess accordingly."
- "Please confirm the corrected balance in writing. I'm prepared to pay any amount I genuinely owe once the bill is verified."

## Escalation

- The provider's **billing department supervisor**, then a formal **billing dispute / appeal** in writing.
- The **insurer's appeals process** (internal, then external review) when coverage is the issue.
- **State insurance commissioner / department of insurance** for insurer misconduct.
- For No Surprises Act violations, the federal **No Surprises Help Desk** / CMS complaint process.
- For large or collections-stage bills, a **medical-billing advocate** or consumer attorney, the dollar amounts often justify it.

Given how often these bills are large and complex, if the situation is high-value or already in collections, tell the person plainly that the letter is a strong first step but they may want a medical-billing advocate or attorney, and point them to the relevant agency above.
