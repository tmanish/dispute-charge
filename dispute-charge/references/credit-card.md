# Credit Card Disputes (FCBA)

The mechanism for credit cards in the US is the **Fair Credit Billing Act (FCBA)**. It is genuinely powerful and gives a person leverage a polite complaint doesn't.

## What the FCBA covers

The FCBA treats these as "billing errors" you can formally dispute:
- Charges you didn't authorize
- Charges for the wrong amount
- Charges for goods or services you ordered but never received (or that weren't delivered as agreed)
- Charges for items returned or not accepted
- Math/accounting errors on the statement
- Failure to credit a payment or return
- Charges you want clarified or want proof of (you can request documentation)

**Important limit to be honest about:** disputes about the *quality* of a delivered good or service are generally **not** "billing errors" under the FCBA's error-resolution procedure. (There's a separate FCBA provision letting you assert claims against the card issuer for quality problems, but it has conditions, often that the purchase was over $50 and made in your home state or within 100 miles of your address. Don't overstate this; if quality is the issue, frame it as a good-faith dispute and note the person may need to pursue it as a claim against the seller.)

## The key facts that make this work

- **The 60-day deadline.** The written dispute must reach the card issuer within 60 days of the date the issuer *sent* the first statement showing the error. This is strict. If the person is inside this window, say so, it's their strongest position. If they're past it, the FCBA error-resolution route may be closed, but a chargeback through the card network may still be possible, and unauthorized-use liability rules still apply, pivot to those rather than citing a deadline they've missed.
- **Send it to the billing-inquiries address, not the payment address.** This is the single most common procedural mistake. The address for "billing inquiries" or "billing errors" is on the statement or the card agreement, not the envelope you mail payments to.
- **Written dispute preserves rights.** A phone call can start things moving but does NOT fully trigger FCBA protections. To be fully protected, the dispute must be in writing and reach the issuer within the 60 days. Many issuers accept disputes through their app or website too; in practice that often works, but for full legal protection, mailing a written notice (certified mail, return receipt) to the billing-inquiries address is the belt-and-suspenders move.

## What the issuer is obligated to do

Once they receive a proper written dispute:
- **Acknowledge** it in writing within 30 days.
- **Resolve** it within two billing cycles (and no more than 90 days).
- They **may not** try to collect the disputed amount, charge finance charges on it, or report it as delinquent to credit bureaus while the dispute is being investigated.
- Your account can't be closed or restricted just for disputing (though the disputed amount may count against your credit limit during the investigation).

These obligations are the leverage. The letter should make clear the person knows them.

## Liability cap for unauthorized charges

For genuinely unauthorized credit-card charges (fraud, stolen card), the cardholder's liability is capped at **$50** under federal law, and most major networks/issuers go further with a $0-liability policy. So for fraud, the person should be confident: their maximum exposure is small, and the letter should state plainly that these charges are unauthorized and they are not liable beyond the statutory cap.

## Language to include in the letter

Weave these in (in plain English, not as a legal recital):
- "I am writing to dispute a billing error on my account under the Fair Credit Billing Act."
- The exact transaction: date, amount, merchant as it appears.
- The reason it's a billing error (pick the right category above).
- "Please correct this error and credit my account for [amount], and send me written confirmation of the correction."
- "As provided under the FCBA, please do not report this disputed amount as delinquent or attempt to collect it while the investigation is ongoing."
- A request for written acknowledgment, and copies of any documents the issuer relied on.

## Escalation if they stall or deny

State the next step calmly in the letter or hold it for a follow-up:
- File a complaint with the **Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (CFPB)** at consumerfinance.gov/complaint, this is the highest-impact escalation for card issuers and banks; companies respond to CFPB complaints because the regulator is watching.
- For issuers that aren't banks, the **FTC** (reportfraud.ftc.gov) takes complaints.
- If the issuer fails to follow FCBA procedures, it forfeits the right to collect the disputed amount (up to $50) even if the charge turns out valid, and the person can sue for damages. For larger amounts, a consumer-law attorney may take the case (sometimes on contingency).
