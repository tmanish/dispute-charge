# Debit Card & Bank Account Disputes (Regulation E / EFTA)

For debit cards and bank-account transactions, the mechanism is the **Electronic Fund Transfer Act (EFTA)**, implemented by **Regulation E**. The protections differ from credit cards in important ways, be precise, because conflating the two leads to wrong advice.

## What Regulation E covers

Unauthorized electronic fund transfers from a consumer's account: debit-card charges, ATM withdrawals, ACH debits, online transfers, and P2P transfers (including ones initiated by a fraudster who tricked the consumer into sharing access). It also covers errors, wrong amounts, transfers that didn't happen as described, computational errors, and a few others.

**Key difference from credit cards:** Reg E is strongest for *unauthorized* transfers and *errors*. It does **not** give the same "item not received / not as described" merchant-dispute power that the FCBA gives credit cards. If someone paid by debit for something that never arrived, they may have a harder time than a credit-card user, they should still contact the bank, but the realistic lever is the bank's own dispute/chargeback process and the merchant directly, not a strong statutory right to non-delivery refunds. Be honest about this rather than implying debit has the same protection as credit.

## The deadlines that determine liability (for unauthorized transfers)

These determine how much money the person can be on the hook for, so they matter enormously:
- **Report within 2 business days** of learning a card was lost/stolen → liability capped at the lesser of **$50** or the amount taken.
- **Report after 2 business days but within 60 days** of the statement → liability can rise to **$500**.
- **Wait more than 60 days** after the statement was sent → potentially **unlimited** liability for transfers after the 60-day mark.

The practical takeaway for the letter: **report immediately**, and if the person is still inside these windows, the letter/notice should be dated and sent now to lock in the lowest liability tier. Note that the bank cannot use the consumer's own "negligence" (e.g. writing a PIN down) to push liability above these caps, that's a common bank pushback that the rules explicitly forbid.

## What the bank is obligated to do after notice of error

This is the leverage:
- **Investigate within 10 business days** of receiving notice.
- If it can't finish in 10 business days, it must **provisionally credit** the disputed amount back to the account so the person has use of the money while the investigation continues (it then has up to 45 days total, longer in some cases).
- If it finds an error occurred, it must **correct it within 1 business day** of that determination.
- If it finds no error, it must send a **written explanation within 3 business days** and provide copies of the documents it relied on, on request.

A bank that blows these timelines is exposed to actual damages, statutory damages, and attorney's fees. The letter should signal the person knows the bank is on a clock.

## How to give notice

- Notice can be oral or written, but **written is far better** for the record. Reg E says the bank must honor an oral notice, but follow it up in writing the same day.
- Send to the bank's dispute/error address (on statements or the bank's site), and keep proof, certified mail with return receipt for high-value disputes, or at minimum a saved copy of the in-app/online dispute submission with date and reference number.

## Language to include

- "I am notifying you of an unauthorized electronic fund transfer / an error on my account under the Electronic Fund Transfer Act and Regulation E."
- Exact transaction(s): date, amount, merchant/description as shown.
- State clearly it was **unauthorized** (or describe the error precisely).
- "Please investigate and credit my account for [amount]. As required under Regulation E, if the investigation is not completed within 10 business days, please provisionally credit the disputed amount to my account."
- Request written findings and copies of any documents relied upon.

## Escalation

- **CFPB complaint** (consumerfinance.gov/complaint) is the strongest escalation for banks, banks respond to these promptly because the regulator tracks them.
- The bank's own internal escalation (ask for a supervisor or the dispute-resolution department, in writing).
- For unresolved high-value cases, a consumer-protection attorney; EFTA allows recovery of attorney's fees, so some take these on contingency.
