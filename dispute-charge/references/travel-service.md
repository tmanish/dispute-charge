# Travel, Hotel, Airline, Rental, Event, and Service Disputes

These cover airlines, hotels, car rentals, event tickets, contractors, and other service purchases. There's usually no single dominant statute the way there is for cards or bank transfers, so the strategy leans on: the company's own contract terms, sector-specific rules where they exist, the payment-method chargeback as a backstop, and public pressure.

## Find the lever for the specific sector

- **Airlines (US):** Strongest specific right is refunds for **cancelled or significantly changed/delayed flights**. US rules require airlines to refund the fare (to the original form of payment, not just a voucher) when the airline cancels or significantly changes a flight and the passenger doesn't accept the alternative. If that's the situation, name it directly, airlines often push vouchers when a cash refund is owed. For lost/delayed bags and other issues, the airline's contract of carriage governs; cite the specific provision.
- **Hotels:** The lever is the **rate, the reservation terms, and what was actually delivered**. Charged a no-show fee on a room you cancelled within policy? Charged for a "resort fee" or damage you dispute? Charged a different rate than booked? The confirmation email and the stated cancellation policy are the evidence. Quote them back.
- **Car rental:** Common disputes are phantom damage charges, fuel charges, and toll/admin fees. The lever is the **check-out/check-in condition documentation** and the rental agreement. Photos at pickup and return are decisive, ask for them and provide your own.
- **Event tickets:** Refund rights usually turn on the **event being cancelled vs. postponed** and the seller's stated policy. Cancelled events generally warrant refunds; postponed is murkier. Cite the policy and the cancellation.
- **Contractors / home services:** The levers are the **written contract/estimate**, work not completed or not to spec, and (in many states) home-improvement consumer-protection statutes and the right to withhold payment for incomplete work. Document the gap between what was agreed and what was delivered.

## The universal backstop: the chargeback

Whatever the sector, almost all of these were paid by card. If the merchant won't refund and the person has a legitimate claim, the **payment-method dispute is the backstop that often actually recovers the money**:
- Credit card → read `credit-card.md`. "Services not rendered" or "not as described" can be a billing-error/chargeback basis (note the FCBA's quality-dispute limits, but card networks' own chargeback rules are often broader than the statute).
- Debit card → read `bank-debit.md`, and set expectations honestly that debit non-delivery protection is weaker.

Run the merchant request first (it builds the record and is required before some chargebacks), then escalate to the card issuer if the merchant stonewalls.

## The facts that win these

- **The booking/contract terms in writing**, confirmation emails, the cancellation policy as it was stated at purchase, the signed estimate.
- **Proof of what was actually delivered vs. promised**, photos, timestamps, the cancellation/change notice the company sent.
- **A clean timeline**, booked on X, company cancelled on Y, requested refund on Z, was offered only a voucher.

## Language to include

- "I'm requesting a refund of [amount] for [booking/confirmation #] dated [date]."
- The specific basis: "[The airline cancelled this flight and I did not accept the rebooking, so I'm entitled to a refund to my original payment method, not a voucher.]" / "[I cancelled within the stated policy window, so the no-show fee is incorrect.]" / "[The work in our signed contract dated X was not completed as specified.]"
- "Please refund [amount] to my original payment method within [10–14 days] and confirm in writing."
- Escalation line: "If unresolved, I'll dispute this charge with my card issuer and file a complaint with [the relevant body below]."

## Escalation

- **Airlines:** US Department of Transportation aviation consumer complaint (the DOT complaint is high-impact, airlines respond).
- **Hotels/rentals/general:** card chargeback, the company's corporate customer-relations (not the front desk), and the **state attorney general** or **Better Business Bureau** for public pressure.
- **Contractors:** state contractor licensing board and consumer-protection office; small-claims court is realistic for these amounts.
- **Card/bank chargeback** as the financial backstop in every case.
