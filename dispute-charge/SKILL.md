# Dispute a Charge

People lose real money every year to wrong charges, junk fees, and denied refunds, not because they don't have a case, but because the letter they write is a polite complaint instead of a request that triggers an actual obligation to act. This skill turns a frustrated person into someone the company has to take seriously.

The goal of every output is a message that does four things a generic complaint doesn't: states the specific facts cleanly, names the mechanism that obligates a response, sets a deadline, and makes the next escalation step visible so the recipient knows ignoring it has a cost.

## The one rule that matters most

**Get the facts before writing the letter.** A vague letter ("you charged me wrong, please fix it") gets a form reply. A specific letter ("on March 3 my account was charged $49.99 by MERCHANT for a subscription I cancelled on February 12, confirmation #A1234") gets resolved. If the person hasn't given you the key facts, ask for them first, but ask efficiently, in one batch, and only for what you actually need. Don't interrogate; gather.

The facts that almost always matter:
- **What** was charged and **how much** (exact amount, exact date)
- **Who** charged it (merchant name as it appears, account/card last 4 if relevant)
- **Why it's wrong** (never received, cancelled, duplicate, wrong amount, not as described, unauthorized)
- **What they've already done** (called? got a confirmation number? been denied? this is the first contact?)
- **What outcome they want** (full refund, partial, correction, removal)

If some facts are missing but the person clearly wants to move fast, write the letter with bracketed placeholders like `[exact date of charge]` so they can fill the gaps, rather than stalling.

## Pick the right mechanism

The single biggest lever is naming the correct consumer-protection mechanism for the situation. This is what separates an effective dispute from a wish. Match the situation to the right reference file and read it before writing, each contains the specific mechanism, the phrases that invoke it, the deadlines involved, and the escalation path.

## Structure of an effective dispute letter

Use this skeleton. Keep it tight, one page is the target. Long letters read as emotional; short letters read as serious.

```
[Your name]
[Your address, needed for formal billing-error disputes]
[Account / card last 4 digits]
[Date]

To: [Company / dispute department]

Re: Dispute of [charge type], [amount] dated [date]

[1. The claim, in one or two sentences. What was charged, when, and that
you are disputing it. State the amount and date precisely.]

[2. The facts. What happened, in plain chronological order. Include any
confirmation numbers, cancellation dates, prior call references, or
evidence you have. This is the spine, be specific and unemotional.]

[3. The mechanism. State the right that applies (from the reference file)
and what it obligates the company to do. This is the sentence that moves
the letter from request to demand.]

[4. The ask + deadline. State exactly what you want (refund the full
[amount] to my account) and by when (within [X] days, per [mechanism]).]

[5. The escalation, stated calmly. Name the next step if unresolved,
chargeback through card issuer, complaint to [relevant body], etc. Don't
threaten; just make the cost of inaction visible.]

[Signature]
```

## Tone: calm, specific, slightly formal

The most effective disputes sound like they were written by someone who knows their rights and fully expects to win, not someone who is angry, and not someone who is begging. Concretely:

- **No insults, no all-caps, no emotional escalation.** Anger gives the recipient a reason to dismiss you. Calm precision doesn't.
- **No over-explaining or apologizing.** "I'm so sorry to bother you but I think there might possibly be a small error" undercuts the whole letter. State the fact: "This charge is incorrect."
- **Cite specifics relentlessly.** Dates, amounts, names, confirmation numbers. Every specific detail signals you have a documented case.
- **One clear ask.** Don't bury the outcome you want. Say it plainly and once.
- **Name the deadline and the next step.** This is the difference-maker. A letter with no deadline can be filed indefinitely; a letter that says "within 30 days, or I will initiate a chargeback" cannot.

## How to deliver the output

Default to giving the person a ready-to-send message they can copy directly, most disputes happen by email, web form, or secure message in an app, so plain copyable text is usually most useful. Offer a printable/mailed version when the mechanism specifically requires writing (some formal billing-error rights are only fully protected when you mail a written dispute to the address the company designates for that purpose, the reference files note when this matters).

If the person seems unsure how to send it, briefly tell them the best channel for their situation (e.g. "for credit-card billing errors, email or in-app is fine to start, but to protect your full rights, also mail it to the billing-inquiries address on your statement").

## What this skill does not do

- It does not invent laws, regulation numbers, or rights. If you're not certain a specific statute applies, describe the mechanism in plain terms ("your card issuer's chargeback process") rather than citing a law you're unsure of. A confidently wrong legal citation can hurt the person's case.
- It does not assume the law is frozen. Consumer rules change, courts vacate rules, agencies rewrite them. If the dispute hinges on a specific rule or a very recent regulation you're not fully certain is current, verify with a quick web search (if available) before citing it, and if you can't verify, describe the mechanism generically rather than asserting the detail.
- It does not give formal legal advice or guarantee outcomes. Where a situation is genuinely complex or high-value (large medical bills, anything involving a lawsuit, debt already sent to collections), say plainly that the letter is a strong first step but the person may want a consumer-law professional or a relevant agency, and point them in that direction.
- It does not help fabricate facts. The whole strategy depends on a truthful, documented account. If someone asks to claim something untrue (e.g. dispute a charge for something they did receive and use), don't help construct a false story, disputing in bad faith can be fraud. Redirect to disputes they actually have grounds for.

## Quick reference: the highest-impact moves

When in doubt, these are the levers that most often unlock a refund:

1. **Name the exact transaction** (date + amount + merchant). Vagueness is the #1 reason disputes fail.
2. **Invoke the right mechanism by name** (chargeback, billing-error dispute, itemized-bill request). Read the matching reference file.
3. **Set a written deadline.** "Within 30 days" beats "as soon as possible" every time.
4. **Make escalation visible.** The recipient should see that the next step (card issuer, regulator, public review) costs them more than just fixing it.
5. **Keep it to one page and zero emotion.** Serious cases read as calm and specific.
